# Deadlift (Conventional) — Beginner's Guide

*Catalog: Seed #6 · Template: v2*

Get the setup right and the lift becomes simple. Get it wrong and your lower back pays.

## Quick cues

- Bar over mid-foot, shins one inch from the bar
- Lats tight, 360° brace before you commit
- Push the floor away — don't pull the bar

---

## Step 1 — Bar Position

- **Bar over mid-foot** — roughly over your shoelaces
- This is non-negotiable. Bar too far forward → you'll pull around your knees instead of straight up.
- **Walk up so your shins are about 1 inch from the bar.** Don't move the bar — move yourself.

---

## Step 2 — Stance

- **Feet:** hip-width apart (narrower than a squat)
- **Toes:** straight forward or turned out 5–15°
- **Weight:** balanced across the whole foot, slight bias toward the heels

---

## Step 3 — Grip

Hinge down (don't squat) and grab the bar just outside your shins.

- **Double overhand** — best for learning, limited by grip strength
- **Mixed grip** (one over, one under) — stronger hold, save for heavier sets
- **Hook grip** — strongest, most uncomfortable at first

> Squeeze the bar like you're trying to bend it.

---

## Step 4 — Set the Body (Bottom-Up Sequence)

Order matters. Do these in sequence every single rep.

1. **Drop hips** until shins touch the bar
2. **Chest up** — proud chest, ribs down (not flared)
3. **Lats engaged** — *"protect your armpits"* or *"squeeze oranges in your armpits"*
4. **Neck neutral** — eyes ~6–10 feet in front of you on the floor
5. **Big breath into your belly + brace hard** — 360° pressure, not just stomach out

> *Right when:* lats engaged feels like your armpits are squeezing the bar back toward you.
> *Wrong when:* shoulders rolled forward, chest collapsed, lower back rounded.

---

## Step 5 — The Pull (Off the Floor)

- **Cue:** *"push the floor away"* — don't think about pulling the bar up
- **Bar drags up the shins** — should be in contact or within an inch
- **Hips and shoulders rise together** — if hips shoot up first, the weight is too heavy or you're not braced
- Keep the bar tight to your body the entire pull

> *Right when:* legs and back rise as one piece; bar tracks up your shins.
> *Wrong when:* hips shoot up first or the bar drifts away from your shins. That's a brace failure or too much weight — reset.

- **B:** *"Squeeze the bar back into your body using your armpits"* — combines lat engagement + bar path
- **I:** *"Push the floor away with your legs"*
- **A:** *"Spread the floor with your feet"* — external, creates hip torque and tightness simultaneously

---

## Step 6 — Lockout (Top of the Lift)

- Stand tall — hips and knees fully extended
- **Squeeze glutes** at the top
- **Don't:** lean back, hyperextend, or shrug the bar up
- Shoulders stay back and down — not rolled forward, not pulled back theatrically

- **B:** *"Squeeze the cheeks to finish"* — direct mind-muscle for the glutes
- **I:** *"Stand tall and stop"*
- **A:** *"Drive hips through the bar"* — external, finishes the lockout without overextending

---

## Step 7 — The Descent

- **Hips back first**, then bend knees once the bar passes them
- Control it down — don't drop, don't bounce
- **Reset every rep:** stand up, breathe, brace, pull. Touch-and-go reps teach bad bracing.

---

## What You Should Feel

Beginners on the deadlift can't tell which muscles are firing. Without that map, they default to lower back. Build the map.

**At setup (bottom position):**
- Lats firing — armpits pulled tight, bar feels "stuck" to your shins
- Hamstrings loaded and stretched
- Core wrapped tight, breath held

**Pulling off the floor:**
- Legs pushing the floor down
- Glutes loading as the bar passes the knees
- Lats holding the bar tight to your body

**At the top:**
- Hard glute squeeze
- Quads locked, knees straight
- Lats still holding shoulders in place

**If you feel this instead → fix this:**
- **Lower back doing all the work** → brace harder, lats tighter, drop the weight; this is the #1 deadlift injury pattern
- **Nothing in the glutes at the top** → think "squeeze a coin" between cheeks at lockout
- **Bicep strain** → mixed grip is overloading the underhand side; switch hands, use straps, or drop the load
- **Knees buckling forward at start** → hips back first, knees track over toes

---

## Mid-set check

> *If something feels off, check your brace first — every deadlift failure mode (back round, hip shoot, bar drift) traces back to a brace that wasn't tight enough.*

---

## Common Mistakes — Fix These First

| Mistake | Severity | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lower back rounding under load | Injury-risk | Weak brace, lats not engaged, or weight too heavy | Drop weight 20%, brace harder, lats tighter, rebuild from a lighter base |
| Hips shoot up before the bar moves | Strength-leak | Quads disengaged off the floor; pulling with the back instead of pushing with the legs | "Push the floor away" — leg drive starts the lift |
| Bar drifts forward away from body | Strength-leak | Lats not engaged | "Squeeze oranges in armpits" cue, slower setup |
| Hyperextending at the top (leaning back) | Injury-risk | Trying to "show" lockout | Stand tall, glutes squeeze, stop |
| Bouncing reps off the floor | Form-only | Rushing | Full reset every rep — stand, breathe, brace, pull |
| Bar slamming down on descent | Injury-risk (over time) | No eccentric control | Hips back first, control to the floor; set down, don't drop |

---

## Red Flags — Stop Immediately

- Lower back pain (not muscle soreness — actual pain, especially sharp or shooting)
- Numbness, tingling, or shooting pain down the legs (potential disc issue)
- Sharp pain in the bicep or sudden weakness on the underhand side of mixed grip (potential bicep tear)
- "Pop" sensation anywhere in the spine, hips, or knees
- Pain that lingers more than 48 hours after the session

---

## Beginner Programming

- **Reps:** 3–6 for strength, 5–8 for technique building
- **Sets:** 3–5
- **Frequency:** 1–2x per week — recovery-heavy lift
- **Placement:** first or second exercise on a pull/lower day
- **Log:** grip used (double-overhand / mixed / hook) — affects how much you can hold vs how much your back can pull

**When to add weight:** hit the top of the rep range across all sets with clean form (no back round, hips and shoulders rising together) → add 5–10 lb.

**When to back off (deload):** form breaks before fatigue (back rounds, hips shoot up), or you fail to hit your bottom rep two sessions in a row → drop weight 10–15% for one week.

---

## Recovery Notes

Heavy deadlifts hit your whole nervous system, not just your back. Most people need **4–7 days** before another heavy session. Light technique work at 50% of working weight can come sooner. Two heavy deadlift sessions in a 4-day window is how you stall, hurt yourself, or both.

If you're sore in your lower back two days later, that's normal. If you're sore for four days, your last session was too much — back off the next one.

## Advanced (Once the Basics Are Automatic)

### Form refinements
- *"Bend the bar around your shins"* — recruits lats and locks bar path
- *"Spread the floor with your feet"* — creates external hip torque
- **Wedge yourself into the bar** — pull the slack out before the lift; the bar should "click" into the plates before you commit
- Pause 2 inches off the floor, then complete the rep — exposes weakness off the floor

### Intensity techniques
- **Deficit deadlifts** — stand on a 1–2 inch plate to extend ROM
- **Paused deadlifts** — 2-second pause just below the knees
- **Block pulls / rack pulls** — partial-ROM overload work
- **Tempo:** 3-second eccentric (controlled descent) — brutal posterior chain stimulus

---

## Plain English

> **Plain English:** Deadlift is a push, not a pull. You're pushing the floor down with your legs while holding the bar still — gravity does the rest. Once that idea clicks, the lift gets way easier on your back, and the weight on the bar starts moving for the right reason.
