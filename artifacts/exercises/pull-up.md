# Pull-Ups (Bodyweight or Weighted) — Beginner's Guide

*Catalog: Seed #7 · Template: v2*

Same setup whether you're using bodyweight or hanging plates from a belt. The hard part isn't the pull — it's learning to feel your back instead of your arms.

## Quick cues

- Active hang every rep — never go passive
- Drive elbows down to back pockets, not "pull up"
- Lower under control — slow eccentric is the rep

---

## Step 1 — Grip Setup

- **Width:** slightly wider than shoulder-width
- **Style:** pronated (palms facing away) — this is a true pull-up
  - Palms facing you = chin-up (more biceps, easier)
  - Neutral grip (palms facing each other) = easiest on shoulders
- **Thumbs:** over the bar or wrapped around — both work, pick what feels secure

---

## Step 2 — The Hang (Starting Position)

The single most-skipped step. Beginners default to a passive dead hang and wonder why their shoulders hurt.

- **Full hang** — arms extended, but **don't go fully passive**
- **Engage shoulders** — pull shoulder blades down and back slightly
- This active hang is the foundation. Every rep starts here, every rep ends here.

> *Right when:* shoulders feel "packed" — pulled away from your ears, traps unloaded.
> *Wrong when:* you can feel weight hanging from your shoulder joints. That's passive hang. Re-engage before you pull.

---

## Step 3 — Set the Body

- **Squeeze glutes** — locks the pelvis
- **Brace core** — stops the swing
- **Legs:** straight down or crossed at the ankles, slight bend at the knees
- **Slight hollow body** — ribs down, body in a gentle banana-shape forward (not arched back)

This kills the kip. If you have to swing to get up, the weight is too heavy or the rep is too hard — fix that, don't compensate.

---

## Step 4 — Adding Weight (If Weighted)

Skip if bodyweight only.

- **Belt:** dipping belt with chain, plates loaded onto the chain
- **Plate position:** in front of you between your legs, not banging your knees
- **Loading:** start with 5 lb. Add 5 lb at a time until you find your working weight.
- **Targets:** bodyweight + 25 lb is a strong intermediate. +45 lb is advanced. +90 lb is elite.

---

## Step 5 — The Pull (Concentric)

- **Lead with the chest** — chest comes to the bar, not chin to the bar
- **Lats do the work**, biceps assist
- **Top position:** chin clearly over the bar, chest as close to the bar as you get
- **No kipping** — no leg swing, no body jerk
- **Breathing:** exhale on the way up

- **B:** *"Pull your elbows down toward your back pockets"* — tells you what muscle group to activate without naming it
- **I:** *"Drive the elbows down to the hips"* — same idea, terser
- **A:** *"Bend the bar in your hands"* — external cue, locks lats and forearms simultaneously

---

## Step 6 — The Descent (Eccentric)

The half of the rep where most growth happens. Most beginners drop. Don't.

- **Tempo:** 2–3 seconds down, controlled
- **End position:** active hang, not passive
- **Breathing:** inhale on the way down

- **B:** *"Lower like you're being timed — slower is harder"*
- **I:** *"Resist the descent, don't surrender to it"*
- **A:** *"Lengthen the lats"* — terse internal, for advanced lifters who already feel their back

---

## Step 7 — Reset Between Reps

- Brief active hang at the bottom — don't go fully limp
- Re-engage shoulders before the next pull
- If you're swinging, pause longer to let it settle. A 2-second pause kills momentum.

---

## What You Should Feel

Most pull-up problems are not strength problems — they're connection problems. If you can't feel your back, you'll never train it.

**At the bottom (active hang):**
- Stretch through the lats (down the sides of your back toward your hips)
- Shoulders engaged but not shrugged
- Forearms loaded, grip firm

**Driving up:**
- Lats firing first — should feel like your back is "pulling your arms down"
- Mid-back squeeze as you near the top
- Biceps assisting, not leading

**At the top:**
- Chest close to the bar
- Full lat contraction — like a hard squeeze you could hold for a beat
- Shoulders still packed, not shrugged into your ears

**If you feel this instead → fix this:**
- **Biceps doing all the work** → drive elbows down toward hips, not back; think "elbows," not "hands"
- **Shoulders straining or pinching** → start every rep from active hang, lower under control, never from a dead hang
- **Lower back arching to "kick" up** → tighten core and glutes before pulling, slight hollow body
- **Forearms cramping before lats fatigue** → grip too tight, or you're hanging from your fingers; recheck grip and active hang

---

## Mid-set check

> *If something feels off, check your hang first — passive hangs are the #1 cause of shoulder and elbow pain on this lift.*

---

## Common Mistakes — Fix These First

| Mistake | Severity | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kipping (swinging body for momentum) | Form-only | Reps too hard or too many | Lower the rep target, do controlled reps only |
| Chin barely clears the bar (looks like a half-rep, feels like an arm grind) | Strength-leak | Pulling with arms, not back | Cue "chest to bar," drive elbows down to hips |
| Shoulders feel strained or pinched | Injury-risk | Passive hang at the bottom; uncontrolled descent | Active hang every rep, lower in 2–3 seconds |
| Elbow pain (medial or lateral) | Injury-risk | Too much volume too fast, or hanging fully passive | Reduce volume, keep shoulders packed, deload one week |
| Stuck halfway up (can't finish the rep) | Strength-leak | Weak top portion of the pull | Add isometric holds at the top of each rep, or band-assisted reps focusing on the top half |
| Forearms fail before back does | Form-only | Hanging from fingers, not whole hand; or grip too tight | Wrap thumbs, distribute load through palm, relax grip slightly until lats are the bottleneck |

---

## Red Flags — Stop Immediately

- Sharp pain in the shoulder joint (not muscle burn — actual joint pain)
- Sharp pain at the elbow (medial = golfer's elbow; lateral = tennis elbow)
- Wrist pain that doesn't resolve between sets
- Numbness or tingling in hands or down the arms
- "Pop" sensation in the shoulder

If any of these show up: stop the set, deload for 1–2 weeks, address the cause (volume, grip width, hang position) before returning.

## Beginner Programming

**Bodyweight:**
- **Reps:** as many as you can with clean form (AMRAP)
- **Sets:** 3–5
- **Frequency:** 2–3x per week — pull-ups recover fast
- **Placement:** first or second exercise on a back/pull day
- **Log:** bodyweight at session — added weight is meaningless without it

**Weighted (after 8+ clean bodyweight reps):**
- **Reps:** 3–6 for strength, 6–10 for size
- **Sets:** 3–5
- **Frequency:** 2x per week
- **Placement:** first exercise on a back day

**When to add weight:** hit 8+ clean bodyweight reps for two consecutive sessions, then start adding 5 lb at a time. With weight on the belt, add 2.5–5 lb when you hit the top of your rep range across all sets, two sessions in a row.

**When to back off (deload):** if form breaks before fatigue (kipping starts, descent uncontrolled), or you fail to hit your bottom rep two sessions in a row, drop weight 10–15% (or remove belt weight) for one week, then resume.

## Can't Do a Pull-Up Yet? Progression Path

Don't skip steps. Each stage has a target — don't move up until you hit it.

1. **Dead hangs** — 3 sets of 30-second active hangs (shoulders packed, not passive)
   → *advance when:* you hold 30 seconds on all 3 sets with shoulders engaged the whole time
2. **Scapular pulls** — hang and pull shoulder blades down and back without bending arms; 3 sets of 8
   → *advance when:* 10 clean reps with a 1-second hold at the top of each
3. **Negatives** — jump or step to the top position, lower yourself slowly (5+ seconds down); 3 sets of 5
   → *advance when:* 5-second controlled descent on all reps without crashing into the bottom
4. **Band-assisted pull-ups** — loop a band over the bar, hook one knee in for assistance; pick band tension that lets you do 6–8 clean reps
   → *advance when:* 10 reps with that band, then drop to a weaker band and restart
5. **Full bodyweight pull-up** — start counting clean reps

Negatives are the fastest single step in this progression. Do them after your main back work, not before.

## Advanced (Once the Basics Are Automatic)

### Form refinements
- *"Pull the bar to your chest, not your chest to the bar"* — flips the mental model, locks the lats harder
- *"Make the bar bend in your hands"* — external cue, recruits lats and forearms simultaneously
- Pause at the top for 1 full second per rep — exposes weak top-end strength
- Eyes slightly above the bar at the top — ensures full ROM, prevents short reps

### Intensity techniques
- **Tempo:** 4-second eccentric, 1-second pause at top
- **Weighted clusters:** 3 reps, rest 15 seconds, 3 reps, rest 15 seconds, 3 reps
- **Rest-pause:** AMRAP set to failure, rest 15 sec, AMRAP again, repeat for a third
- **Partials at the top** after main sets — work the hardest range of motion under fatigue
- **L-sit pull-ups** — legs held in front for added core demand and zero kip possibility

---

## Plain English

Pull-ups aren't an arm exercise. You're using your back to bring your elbows down — your hands just happen to be holding the bar. Once that idea clicks, you'll start feeling muscles in your back you didn't know existed, and the lift gets easier even before you get stronger.

> **Plain English:** The bar is a handle. Your back is the engine. Stop trying to lift yourself with your arms.
