# Squat (High-Bar Back Squat) — Beginner's Guide

*Catalog: Seed #11 · Template: v2*

The hard part isn't standing up — it's keeping the bar from folding you forward on the way down.

## Quick cues

- Build the shelf, brace before you unrack
- Hips and chest rise together — never hips first
- Push the floor away with the whole foot

---

## Step 1 — Bar Setup in the Rack

The most-skipped detail. A bar racked too high or too low costs you tightness before you've even unracked.

- **Bar height:** just below shoulder level. You should bend your knees slightly to unrack — never tiptoe up to it.
- **Walk under the bar:** square up, both feet under, then unrack with both legs at once.
- **J-hooks / safeties:** set safeties at the bottom of your squat depth — high enough to catch a failed rep, low enough you don't bang them mid-set.

> *Wrong when:* you have to stand on your toes to clear the bar. Reset the pins before you load.

---

## Step 2 — Stable Base (Stance and Foundation)

Built from the ground up. Feet → glutes → core → head.

1. **Feet:** shoulder-width to slightly wider, toes turned out 15–30°
2. **Tripod foot:** weight spread across heel, big-toe ball, pinky-toe ball — never let any of the three lift
3. **Glutes:** pre-engage before liftoff
4. **Core:** big breath into the belly, brace like you're about to take a punch
5. **Head:** neutral spine, eyes on a fixed spot ~6 feet ahead — not up at the ceiling

Walk out: 2–3 steps, no more. Foot setup wastes energy.

> Think: *feet rooted, brace locked, eyes steady — then move.*

---

## Step 3 — Bar Position and Upper Back

The "shelf" makes high-bar work. No shelf = bar slides, upper back collapses, lift fails.

- **Bar position:** on top of the upper traps, just below the C7 vertebra (the bony bump at the base of your neck). NOT on the neck itself.
- **Build the shelf:** pull shoulder blades down and back hard — the squeeze creates the muscle pad the bar sits on.
- **Chest up, ribs DOWN.** Flared ribs = lost brace.
- **Elbows pulled forward and under the bar** — keeps upper back tight.

> *Right when:* the bar feels welded to your back. You can't tell where the bar ends and your traps begin.
> *Wrong when:* the bar rolls or slides during the descent. You lost upper-back tightness — fix it before you load heavier.

---

## Step 4 — Grip

- **Width:** as narrow as your shoulder mobility allows. Tighter grip = tighter upper back.
- **Style:** thumbs over the bar (safer for high-bar). Some lifters prefer thumbs wrapped — both work, pick what feels secure.
- **Squeeze hard.** Grip tension feeds back into upper-back tension.

---

## Step 5 — The Descent (Eccentric)

The half of the lift where most beginners lose the bar. Slow it down.

- **Tempo:** 2–3 seconds, controlled
- **Depth:** hip crease at or below the top of the knee (parallel or just below). Going deeper is fine *if* you can keep a neutral spine.
- **Knees:** track over the toes, in line with your foot angle. Don't let them cave inward.
- **Breathing:** hold the brace from the top. Don't exhale until you stand back up.

- **B (Beginner):** *"Sit between your heels"* — gives a clear visual destination
- **I (Intermediate):** *"Knees track over toes, hips drop straight down"*
- **A (Advanced):** *"Spread the floor with your feet"* — external cue, locks glutes and abductors

---

## Step 6 — The Drive (Concentric)

- **Initiation:** chest and hips rise *together* — NOT hips first
- **Path:** bar travels straight up over the midfoot. If it drifts forward, you're losing the bar.
- **Lockout:** stand fully — knees straight, glutes squeezed, ribs still down
- **Breathing:** exhale at or just past the sticking point

- **B:** *"Drive your back up into the bar"*
- **I:** *"Push the floor away with your whole foot"*
- **A:** *"Elbows up through the lift"* — keeps chest tall and bar over midfoot

---

## What You Should Feel

Most failed squats aren't leg failures — they're brace or upper-back failures. Knowing what *correct* feels like is half the lift.

**At the bottom (in the hole):**
- Quads loaded, glutes stretched
- Brace tight in the belly — like a beach ball pushing out against a belt
- Upper back still tight, bar still welded

**Driving up:**
- Quads + glutes fire together
- Upper back stays braced — no slumping forward
- Bar moves over the midfoot, not in front

**At the top:**
- Glutes locked
- Brace held until you exhale
- Bar still on the shelf, no shifting

**If you feel this instead → fix this:**
- **Lower back rounding ("butt wink") at the bottom** → reduce depth to where your back stays neutral, work hip mobility outside the lift
- **Falling forward as you stand** → brace harder, push knees out earlier, cue "elbows up"
- **Knees caving inward** → cue "spread the floor"; light band around knees on warmup sets
- **Bar feels heavy on your neck** → bar is sitting on cervical spine, not traps; build the shelf harder before you unrack

---

## Mid-set check

> *If something feels off, check your brace first — almost every failed squat is a bracing failure, not a leg failure.*

---

## Common Mistakes — Fix These First

| Mistake | Severity | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hips shoot up before the bar moves (looks like a "good morning" out of the hole) | Strength-leak | Quads weak relative to back | Pause squats at parallel; cue "drive chest up" |
| Knees cave inward | Injury-risk | Weak glutes / abductors | "Spread the floor" cue; band around knees on warmups |
| Butt wink (lower back rounds at the bottom) | Injury-risk | Squatting below your real mobility limit | Squat to where your back stays neutral; work hip mobility outside the lift |
| Heels rise off the floor | Form-only / Strength-leak | Tight ankles or weight too forward | Use flat-soled or heeled shoes; drop weight; ankle mobility work |
| Bar drifts forward over the toes | Strength-leak / Injury-risk | Lost upper-back tightness mid-rep | Re-rack, rebuild the shelf; lighter weight to learn the groove |
| Falling forward at the bottom | Injury-risk | Brace failed mid-rep | Brace harder before descent; cue elbows up |

---

## Red Flags — Stop Immediately

- Sharp pain in the lower back (not muscle burn — actual pain)
- Knee pain, especially medial (inside) — usually a knee-cave warning sign
- Sudden loss of brace mid-rep ("the wind got knocked out of me")
- "Pop" sensation anywhere — hip, knee, back
- Light-headedness right after a set — over-bracing, exhale earlier next time

If any show up: re-rack the bar, deload 1–2 weeks, fix the cause before returning.

---

## Beginner Programming

- **Reps:** 5 for strength, 6–10 for size
- **Sets:** 3–5
- **Frequency:** 1–2x per week — squats are systemically taxing
- **Placement:** first big lift on a leg day, fresh

**When to add weight:** hit the top of your rep range × all sets × two sessions in a row → add 5–10 lb.

**When to back off (deload):** if form breaks before fatigue (butt wink, knee cave, bar drift), or you fail to hit your bottom rep two sessions in a row → drop weight 10–15% for one week, then resume.

---

## Recovery Notes

Heavy squats hit your entire system — legs, lower back, CNS. Most people need 3–5 days between heavy squat sessions. Light technique work at 50% × 5×3 can come sooner. If you're walking like a robot for 4+ days, your volume is too high or your novel-exercise tolerance is too low — back off, don't push through.

---

## Advanced (Once the Basics Are Automatic)

### Form refinements
- *"Push the floor away with your whole foot"* — external cue, locks quads and glutes
- *"Elbows up through the lift"* — keeps chest tall, bar over midfoot
- Pause for 1 second at the bottom every rep — exposes weak depth and trains the brace

### Intensity techniques
- **Tempo:** 4-second eccentric, 1-second pause at the bottom
- **Pause squats** (2-second pause in the hole) — kills stretch reflex, builds raw strength out of the bottom
- **Cluster sets:** 3 reps, rest 30 sec, repeat 3–4×
- **Box squats** — sit fully onto a box, pause, stand. Resets every rep, exposes leg strength.
- **Rest-pause AMRAP** for hypertrophy work on a back-off set

---

## Plain English

The squat isn't a leg exercise — it's a whole-body brace test that happens to use your legs. Your legs do the visible work, but if your brace fails or your upper back collapses, the bar wins. Get the brace and the shelf right, and the legs do the rest.

> **Plain English:** Build a tight torso. Sit straight down. Stand straight up. Don't let the bar bend you forward.
